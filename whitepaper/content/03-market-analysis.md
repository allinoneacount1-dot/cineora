# 2. Market Analysis

Cineora sits at the intersection of three large, measurable industries. Sizing them honestly — using named, dated sources — matters because the protocol's claims about scale must survive scrutiny rather than rely on speculative future projections.

## 2.1 Total Addressable Market — Entertainment, Media, and the Creator Economy

PwC's *Global Entertainment & Media Outlook 2024–2028* estimates global entertainment and media revenue at approximately **USD 2.8 trillion in 2024**, projected to reach **USD 3.4 trillion by 2028** at a compound annual growth rate of roughly 5.4%. This includes film, television, streaming, gaming, music, publishing, and internet advertising. It is the most conservative envelope within which Cineora can plausibly participate.

Layered on top of that is the creator economy proper. Goldman's *Creator Economy Could Be a $480B+ Market* (May 2023) projects the creator economy growing from roughly **USD 250 billion in 2023 to USD 480 billion by 2027**, defined as the aggregate revenue earned by individual creators across platforms, tooling, and brand deals. Adobe's *Future of Creativity Report* (2022) corroborates this trajectory, estimating **USD 191 billion in 2022** with a similar five-year curve.

## 2.2 AI Generation — A New Layer, Not a Replacement

Grand View Research's *Artificial Intelligence in Media & Entertainment* report values the segment at approximately **USD 14.8 billion in 2023**, projected to expand at a **24.5% CAGR to USD 99.5 billion by 2030**. McKinsey's *Economic Potential of Generative AI* (June 2023) places the broader generative AI opportunity at **USD 2.6 to 4.4 trillion annually** across sixty-three identified use cases, with marketing, sales, software engineering, and creative content among the highest-value buckets.

What the McKinsey figure obscures is that the *capture* of this value will be highly skewed. Capital expenditure on raw model inference (Nvidia, hyperscalers, foundation model providers) will absorb the majority. Application-layer platforms — the layer at which Cineora operates — capture the residual but durable share tied to workflow ownership and community.

## 2.3 Serviceable and Serviceable-Obtainable Markets

Cineora's serviceable addressable market (SAM) is best defined as the **on-chain cultural asset layer**: digital art, music NFTs, generative art collections, and protocol-owned IP. Statista values the global NFT market at approximately **USD 26.9 billion in 2023**, with a more conservative 2024–2028 outlook following the post-2021 cycle correction. Within this envelope, the subset of assets that are **persistent, state-bearing, and economically active** (rather than static JPEG collectibles) is estimated at well under 5% of total NFT volume, or roughly **USD 1.0–1.5 billion annually today**.

The serviceable obtainable market (SOM) for Cineora over a five-year horizon is therefore plausibly **USD 250–750 million in annual protocol revenue** at maturity, anchored to a recurring royalty share of the cinematic IP it incubates rather than to NFT speculation. This is the number against which the token economy in Section 6 is sized.

## 2.4 Why This Market, Why Now

Three structural shifts make the window specific to 2026–2028:

1. The collapse of generative video cost curves (Sora, Veo, Kling) has shifted the binding constraint from *production* to *continuity*.
2. Solana's maturation — Firedancer client beta, Token Extensions, state compression — has made persistent on-chain worlds operationally viable rather than theoretical.
3. Creator-economy platforms (Patreon, Substack, OnlyFans, Twitch) have reached saturation and are now competing on margin rather than growth, creating an opening for a substrate that grants creators economic sovereignty instead of platform rent.

Cineora is positioned to serve this opening without claiming to displace any of the incumbents named above.
